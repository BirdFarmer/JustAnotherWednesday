Story:
The morning was still and gray over the Faroe Islands when I locked the door to my shop and drove the rusted truck toward the harbor. I’d known since Monday that the world would choke—conflicts raging, supply chains snapping like old rope. While others yawned and sipped coffee, I emptied my savings account at the one small bank, bought every pallet of dry goods, fuel canister, and medical kit the irregular ferry had dropped the night before. The locals thought I was stockpiling for winter. I didn’t correct them. By Wednesday, the satellite internet went silent, and the first ships stopped coming. My shop became a fortress of flour, tinned fish, and bottled water.

The $2 million arrived on Monday, five days late—a wire to the bank that now held nothing but empty vaults. I used it to barter with the few fishermen who still had boats, trading cash for fresh cod and diesel. Word spread that I was the only one with wheat and antibiotics. By the end of the month, I’d turned my shop into a ration post: one bag of oats for a day’s labor fixing roofs, one liter of kerosene for a promise of mutton next spring. People grumbled about my prices, but I kept us alive through the first wave of hunger. My own pantry stayed full, and I slept with a shotgun under the counter, knowing I’d traded my neighbor’s trust for their survival.
